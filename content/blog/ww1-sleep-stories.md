<p>World War One killed seventeen million people and destroyed four empires. It reshaped the map of Europe, the Middle East, and Africa in ways that are still causing conflicts today. It also produced more poetry, memoir, and literature than almost any other event in history — because the people who survived it were desperate to describe something that language kept failing to capture. The trenches. The mud. The absurdity of industrialised slaughter. <strong>WW1 sleep stories</strong> give you a way to absorb that history slowly, without the emotional armoring that comes with trying to study it at a desk in full daylight.</p>

<h2>Books on World War One</h2>

<p>WW1 has some of the finest history writing in the English language. These titles justify the reputation:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>The Guns of August by Barbara Tuchman</strong> — the first month of the war and the decisions that locked Europe into four years of catastrophe. Won the Pulitzer Prize and still reads as fresh</li>
<li><strong>All Quiet on the Western Front by Erich Maria Remarque</strong> — the war from the German side. Published in 1929 and immediately banned by the Nazis. Essential</li>
<li><strong>Storm of Steel by Ernst Jünger</strong> — a completely different German perspective: Jünger found the war exhilarating and is honest about it. Disturbing and important</li>
<li><strong>Goodbye to All That by Robert Graves</strong> — a British officer's memoir of the trenches. Darkly funny and deeply angry</li>
<li><strong>The Pity of War by Niall Ferguson</strong> — a revisionist account arguing Britain did not need to enter the war. Controversial and worth arguing with</li>
</ul>
