<p>Mythology was always meant to be heard in the dark. The great mythological traditions — Greek, Norse, Celtic, Egyptian, Mesopotamian — emerged from oral cultures where stories were told around fires at night, passed from one generation to the next by voice. The written versions we have now are late transcriptions of something that was fundamentally a spoken, nighttime tradition. Listening to mythology as you fall asleep is not a modern trick. It is the original format.</p>

<h2>Egyptian Mythology: Gods Built Into the Landscape</h2>

<p>Egyptian mythology cannot be separated from Egyptian geography. The Nile's annual flood was Osiris dying and returning. Every natural phenomenon had a divine explanation, and the divine world was not separate from the physical one — it was the physical one, understood differently. <h2>Books on World Mythology</h2>

<p>These are the books that serious mythology readers keep returning to:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Mythology by Edith Hamilton</strong> — the single most accessible and reliable introduction to Greek, Roman, and Norse mythology in English. Still essential seventy years after publication</li>
<li><strong>The Hero with a Thousand Faces by Joseph Campbell</strong> — the structural argument for a universal monomyth underlying all world mythologies. Influential enough to have shaped Star Wars, among other things</li>
<li><strong>Norse Mythology by Neil Gaiman</strong> — a retelling of the Norse myths by one of the best storytellers working today. Preserves the strangeness while making it accessible</li>
<li><strong>The Egyptian Book of the Dead translated by E.A. Wallis Budge</strong> — the primary source for Egyptian mythology and the underworld journey. Demanding but irreplaceable</li>
<li><strong>The Prose Edda by Snorri Sturluson</strong> — the foundational Norse mythology text, compiled in thirteenth-century Iceland. The source for most of what we know about the Norse gods</li>
</ul>
